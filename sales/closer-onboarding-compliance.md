**Value-ladder framing (honest arithmetic):** the longer the commitment, the lower the effective monthly
rate *and* the more time for results to compound. Use that to move a hesitant parent **up** rather than
down. Anchor value to **teacher quality, the 1:1 format, twice-weekly consistency, and measured progress**
— never to a guaranteed outcome.

**Commitment framing (Meg's view):** the 6- and 12-month terms exist because this is **not an easy fix.**
Families who come in month-to-month and bail actually waste their money — a child only moves forward with
consistency over time. Use the longer terms to hook families into real progress, not to squeeze them.

## 2. Program facts
- **Format:** twice-a-week, one-to-one sessions (not group), per Meg's system.
- **Session length:** 50 minutes per session → **two 50-minute sessions/week = 100 minutes of intervention.
  This is reading intervention, NOT homework help.** Say "50-minute sessions" clearly and more than once
  (see the reminder-email quirk below).
- **Reminder-email quirk to pre-empt:** the booking system (**TutorCruncher**) blocks the session as a full
  hour, so the parent's reminder email *looks* like an hour. The tutor is paid the full hour (10 min prep
  built in), but **face-to-face time is 50 minutes.** Clarify this so parents aren't confused later.
- **Assessment-first:** the very first lesson is an assessment (platform: **MOI Max**). It goes all the way
  back to pre-K to find every gap and **documents where the child starts** (e.g., a 3rd grader reading at a
  1st-grade level = a 2-year gap). Kept low-pressure so it doesn't dent the child's confidence.
- **Re-assessment ~every 4 months** with the same assessment, so growth is measured and documented, not
  assumed (e.g., "gap closed here, still work here, now reading at 2nd grade").
- **Not scripted — this is the core differentiator.** We are NOT a scripted program (e.g., Brighterly).
  Each tutor brings a **toolbox** and adapts to the child, because struggling readers are often bored
  learners who've never found something that engages them. Tutors are trained in **Wilson, Orton, and UFLI**
  and apply years of classroom experience to the individual child.
- **Teachers:** certified, experienced classroom teachers — **very senior, many with master's degrees and
  ~25 years' experience, and specialized backgrounds in reading and related fields (speech, ABA, dyslexia).**
  Not high-school students or gig tutors.
- **Consistency:** same teacher, same days/times each week; reminder emails before lessons.
- **Lesson reports after every session** (AI listens and drafts, the tutor edits before it goes out):
  covers the child's attitude, whether they struggled, what the tutor worked on, and historical notes.
  These reports **handle ~99% of parent questions** — set the expectation that this, not calls before/after
  each lesson, is how they stay informed.
- **Parents don't dictate lessons.** Tutors design lessons from the assessment; parents trust the tutor's
  expertise. Set this upfront (it prevents the hardest-to-manage clients). Parents *may* upload an IEP/504
  to their account, but **tutors deliberately take a fresh look** and don't rely on those documents.
- **Reading-intervention timeline: we tell parents ~16 weeks.** (Research shows measurable gains at
  ~10–12 weeks — see §3 — but **we deliberately frame it as 16 weeks to under-promise.** Don't promise
  faster.) Gains are cumulative.
- **Honest caveat on speed:** a child with an **intellectual disability** (especially an IEP *plus* an
  intellectual disability) will take longer to progress. Say so honestly.
- **Executive-functioning assessment:** a skills profile (time management, flexible thinking, reasoning,
  memory, etc.) evaluating 12 academic skills; **recommended grade 3 and above.** **Not built into the
  price — a one-time $125 assessment, done at home by the family (not by the tutor)**, via **Mindprint
  Learning.** **Decision: shelve it — don't pitch it by default** (parents find it interesting but
  overwhelming). Sell it later only if a family clearly needs it.
- **Reach:** delivered online; we have students **all over, including American families based
  internationally.** *(Exact current state count: still to confirm.)*
- **Scale:** ~**32 certified teachers** on the team (see §3B for families served).

### Understanding the caller (fast positioning)
Callers are almost always **moms**, and they generally fall into one of three buckets:
1. **Worried, but the school says "he's fine / not bad enough."** The child is *right above intervention*, so
   the school won't provide services — but the parent knows their kid is slipping and nobody's helping.
2. Child has a **504 plan.**
3. Child has an **IEP** (school has already identified them as below grade level in reading).

Common threads: **early speech issues** (which affect reading outcomes), **trauma**, or **adoption**.
Surprising one to know: **private-school kids often have NO services** (no reading specialists) — private
school is *assumed* to offer more but is often backwards for a struggling reader.

**Qualifying / when to walk:** watch for the parent (Meg's "international-mom" pattern is the usual example)
whose child **isn't actually behind** and who wants to **dictate lessons, demand curriculum/homework, or talk
to the tutor before and after every session.** Set expectations upfront; if they can't accept how the
program works, it's okay to filter them out gracefully. A closer willing to disqualify protects the program.

## 3. Usable statistics
Two buckets. **Use the external research freely (attributed as research). Never state an internal number
without real records to back it.**

### A) External research — cleared to cite (as "research shows…", not as our results)
- **Dyslexia is common:** roughly **15–20% of people — about 1 in 5** — have some degree of dyslexia
  (International Dyslexia Association). Most common learning disability, ~**70–80% of people with reading
  difficulties.** *Use it to normalize the parent's situation.*
- **One-on-one beats group:** research consistently finds **1:1 tutoring produces larger gains than
  small-group tutoring.**
- **Tutoring works, measurably:** a large meta-analysis found an overall effect of ~**0.37 standard
  deviations — roughly moving a student from the 50th to the 66th percentile.**
- **What drives results:** research credits **consistency, a structured curriculum, and qualified tutors** —
  exactly our model.
- **Timeline:** measurable reading gains typically appear after **~10–12 weeks** of consistent instruction.
  **Note:** we tell *parents* ~16 weeks to under-promise (see §2) — cite the 10–12-week figure only as
  general research, and set the family's expectation at 16.

**On the "high-dosage" research — read carefully.** Our **twice-weekly** model sits at the **lower edge** of
what studies call "high-dosage" tutoring (~2–3+ sessions/week, strongest effects at 3+/week, often
in-school). You **may** describe the program as *frequent, consistent, research-aligned* tutoring. You may
**not** cite the popular *"double or triple learning gains"* headline as an expected outcome — that figure is
tied to the most intensive 3+/week programs and would overstate what a parent is buying.

*Sources for verification: International Dyslexia Association; University of Chicago Education Lab / Saga
Education; J-PAL and What Works Clearinghouse meta-analyses.*

### B) Internal numbers — cleared to state (verified by Meg)
- **Families served / scale:** **750+ families** to date.
- **Renewal / retention:** **most families stay year-round.**
- **Team:** ~**32 certified teachers** — very senior, many with master's degrees and specialized backgrounds
  in reading and related fields (**speech, ABA, dyslexia**).
- **Named case studies / testimonials:** refer parents to the **website** (only use named stories with
  written parent consent).

## 6. Founder's notes & pro tips (from Dr. Meg)
*Meg has taken hundreds of these calls — this is the part no script can capture.*

- **The question that most reliably gets a parent to open up:** Keep it simple. Say hi, ask what **state**
  they're calling from (you need the time zone), then: **"Tell me about your kiddo."** Don't talk about
  yourself — take notes, get the child's name, ask public vs. private school, and listen for the journey.
- **What turns "let me think about it" into a yes:** At the end of every call, ask if they'd like you to
  **send 3–4 tutors to consider.** Assume you're moving forward, agree to **text the options by end of day,
  and follow up within 24 hours** — so you stay in touch and show you're committed to their child.
- **The objection you hear most — price:** *"Pay now, or pay for the rest of the kiddo's life."* Meg also
  frames tutoring as **"like a car payment"** — a manageable monthly commitment, not a luxury.
- **The "does online really work?" objection:** *"To date, no teacher has gotten your child to level — so
  think about that. Changing up how we deliver it is exactly what tends to work."*
- **Warm up every booking:** Text **every single booking** and start building the relationship right away —
  ask for confirmation so they feel warm by the time they arrive on the Zoom.
- **Tone & pacing with anxious parents:** Just **listen.** They're worried, not difficult — they've never
  found the right help and they *want* to trust you. You're the answer to their prayers, literally. Don't
  rush to pitch over the emotion.
- **The tutor-match step is non-negotiable:** We talk it through because **the parent helps pick the tutor.**
  Keep this step — it's what makes us unique, and it's why we rarely have to reassign tutors.
