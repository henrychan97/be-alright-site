# Be Alright Tutoring — Closer Interview Guide

A structured guide for interviewing high-ticket closers. The first sections are standard questions; the
**role-play section is the real test** — that's where you see how someone actually handles a warm, anxious
parent. Each role-play includes lines to read aloud and green/red flags so you can score candidates
consistently.

**What you're screening for:** because you're selling a kids' reading program to emotionally invested
parents, the best closer isn't the one who can "overcome any objection." It's the one who closes well
**and** doesn't oversell — because over-promised results on a child's education turn into refunds,
chargebacks, and bad word-of-mouth. Hold that lens through the whole interview.

## 1. Background & track record
1. Walk me through your closing experience — what offers, what price points, what call format?
2. What's the highest-ticket thing you've personally closed, and what was that sale like?
3. What were your numbers in your last closing role — close rate, average order value, and roughly what you
   took home?
4. How many calls a day were you running, and what does your ideal volume look like? *(This seat is 4–5
   pre-booked calls/day.)*
5. Walk me through your typical call structure, start to finish.
6. What does your follow-up process look like for someone who doesn't buy on the first call?

## 2. Sales approach & philosophy
1. These are emotional buyers — parents worried about their kid. How does selling to an emotional buyer
   differ from selling to, say, a business owner?
2. Tell me about a time you *didn't* close someone on purpose. Why?
3. How do you handle a price objection without dropping the price?
4. Where's the line for you between persistence and pressure? How do you know when you've crossed it?
5. A prospect is clearly excited but you can tell they can't really afford it. What do you do?
6. How do you handle it when someone wants a result you're not sure the program can deliver?

## 3. Behavioral / situational
1. Tell me about your best close ever — what made it work?
2. Tell me about a deal you lost that you should have won. What went wrong?
3. Describe a time a customer came back unhappy after you closed them. How did you handle it, and what did
   you change afterward?
4. How do you keep your energy and consistency across call #1 and call #5 on a heavy day?
5. How do you stay organized with pre-booked calls, notes, and follow-ups? What's your stack?

## 4. Fit for this specific offer
1. What questions would you want answered about our program before you'd feel confident selling it?
2. How would you explain the value of a premium 1:1 reading program to a parent comparing it to a $40/hour
   local tutor?
3. What would make you walk away from this role six months in?

## 5. Role-play scenarios
**How to run these:** Tell the candidate they're taking a warm inbound call — the parent has already
applied, answered qualifying questions, and watched the full presentation. You play the parent; read the
opening line, then throw the curveball when it fits. Let it run 5–8 minutes each. Run at least two
(Scenario A is the must-do).

### Scenario A — The "I need to talk to my spouse" stall
**Parent persona:** Mom of a 9-year-old reading well below grade level. Engaged, warm, clearly wants this —
but hesitating at the end.
**Opening line (read aloud):** "This sounds amazing, it really does. I just… I think I need to talk to my
husband before we commit to something this big."
**Curveball (if they push too hard or too soft):** "I just don't want to make a big decision like this on
the spot, you know?"
**Green flags:** Explores whether it's a real second-decision-maker or a soft "no"; gets specific about what
the spouse would need to know; offers to include the spouse rather than bulldozing past them; keeps the
child's timeline front and center without guilt-tripping.
**Red flags:** Steamrolls the objection; manufactures fake urgency ("this price disappears tonight"); makes
the parent feel guilty for "delaying help for your child"; folds immediately and ends the call with no next
step.

### Scenario B — The burned-before skeptic
**Parent persona:** Dad who's already spent money on a tutoring center and an app, with nothing to show for
it. Guarded, a little cynical.
**Opening line:** "Look, we've already tried Kumon and one of those reading apps and honestly it was a
waste of money. What makes this any different?"
**Curveball:** "How do I know this isn't going to be the same thing?"
**Green flags:** Gets curious about what specifically failed before; differentiates honestly without
trashing competitors; sets realistic expectations rather than promising a miracle; uses proof/specifics
over hype.
**Red flags:** Over-promises ("your son will be reading at grade level in 30 days, guaranteed"); badmouths
competitors; gets defensive; leans on pressure instead of rebuilding trust.

### Scenario C — The overwhelmed, emotional parent
**Parent persona:** Mom who's near tears — her daughter is starting to say she's "stupid" and dreads
school. Highly emotional, motivated, vulnerable.
**Opening line:** "Sorry, this is just… it's been really hard. She used to love school and now she cries
about it. I just want my daughter back."
**Curveball:** Stay emotional. If they rush to pitch, get quieter and more upset.
**Green flags:** Slows down; lets the parent be heard before selling anything; validates without exploiting
the emotion; doesn't weaponize the child's pain to push the close. This scenario tests whether they can sit
with emotion and still guide the call — not whether they can capitalize on a crying parent.
**Red flags:** Pushes the pitch over the emotion; uses the daughter's distress as leverage ("so you
understand why you can't afford to wait"); robotic empathy followed by an immediate price ask; or the
opposite — gets so flustered they never guide the call at all.

### Scenario D — The wrong-fit / can't-afford-it call
**Parent persona:** Kind, eager parent who clearly wants it but keeps signaling money is genuinely tight —
mentions a payment plan won't really work, asks about financing twice.
**Opening line:** "I really want to do this for him. Is there any way we could split it into smaller
payments? Money's just really tight right now."
**Curveball:** "I could maybe put part of it on a credit card…"
**Green flags:** Doesn't push someone into debt for a premium program; honestly assesses fit; offers the
legitimate options that exist (and only those); is willing to disqualify gracefully and protect both the
parent and your refund rate. **This is the integrity test** — a closer who'll sell to anyone is a liability
with this offer.
**Red flags:** Pushes the credit card; ignores clear affordability signals; closes the sale anyway. A
candidate who "wins" this role-play by closing it should worry you, not impress you.

## 6. Wrap-up & scoring
**Closing questions for the candidate:**
- What did you think of that role-play — what would you have done differently?
- What's your read on our offer now? Would you be comfortable selling it?
- What do you need from us to hit your numbers in this seat?

**Quick scoring rubric (1–5 each):**

| Dimension | What you're rating |
|---|---|
| Discovery / listening | Did they uncover the real situation before pitching? |
| Holds price with empathy | Defended value without pressure or panic discounting |
| Honesty / realistic expectations | Avoided over-promising results on a child |
| Emotional intelligence | Handled vulnerable parents with genuine care |
| Integrity / willingness to disqualify | Walked away from a wrong-fit close |
| Video presence & energy | Warm, clear, and natural on camera |

**Strongest signal overall:** a candidate who closes confidently **and** chose not to close Scenario D.
That combination is rare and exactly what this seat needs.
